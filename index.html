Preparing for Emergencies

Setting Yourself Up for Success in a Crisis

üß† The Golden Hour Mindset

Emergencies trigger human panic, which amplifies a dog's stress response and can turn a manageable situation life-threatening. Preparation creates procedural memory ‚Äî your body knows what to do when your mind is racing.

A comprehensive first aid kit is your first line of defense in canine emergencies

üéØ Pre-Emergency Conditioning

  • Practice restraint during calm times so your dog accepts handling
  • Desensitize to transportation: Ensure your dog enters a vehicle calmly, even when distressed
  • Know your baseline: Record normal vitals quarterly (see Topic 3)
  • Emergency reconnaissance: Locate 24-hour ER vets within a 30-minute radius; save their numbers under "Vet ER - [Location]"

üéí The Portable Kit

  • Digital rectal thermometer (+ petroleum jelly)
  • Self-adhering bandage wrap (Vetrap/Coban)
  • Gauze pads, non-stick sterile dressing, rolled gauze
  • Emergency muzzle or 3-foot gauze/belt
  • Tick remover tool
  • Saline eye wash
  • Styptic powder (for nail bleeds)
  • Emergency thermal blanket (space blanket)
  • Collapsible water bowl & latex gloves
  • Flashlight/headlamp
  • OTC 3% hydrogen peroxide*
  • Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) ‚Äî 1mg/lb
  • Emergency contact card with vitals

*For inducing vomiting ONLY on veterinary advice

üè† The Home Station

All portable kit items PLUS:

  • Activated charcoal (vet-grade, not BBQ variety)
  • Digital scale (for medication dosages)
  • Nail clippers, scissors, blunt-tipped bandage scissors
  • Soft Elizabethan collar (prevents wound interference)
  • Orthopedic foam blocks/splints (various sizes)
  • Clean towels (compression, restraint, warmth)

Approach, Capture & Restraint

Safety First: Protecting You and the Injured Dog

Learn to recognize signs of fear and aggression before approaching an injured dog

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Warning

The Psychology of Pain: Even the sweetest family dog can bite when injured. Pain overrides training and social conditioning. Never assume your dog "would never bite."

üö∂ Approach Protocol

1

Assess the Environment

Secure loose traffic, electrical wires, or other hazards first. Your safety comes first.

2

Observe from 10 Feet

Is the dog lateral recumbent? Vocalizing? Guarding a limb? Assess body language before approaching.

3

Approach Indirectly

Come from the side (not head-on), avoid direct eye contact, use low voice tones.

4

Offer Scent

Extend a closed fist for the dog to sniff if conscious; let them come to you if mobile.

5

Touch Last

Begin with the shoulders/ribcage area, avoiding the head initially.

ü§ù Capture Techniques

Rabies Pole/Makeshift: Use a leash looped to create a noose if the dog is aggressive; tighten behind ears, not throat.

Burrito Method: For small dogs, wrap securely in a towel with the head exposed to prevent thrashing.

Blanket Toss: For severely aggressive dogs, drop a heavy blanket over the head to disorient, then scoop.

ü§ê Muzzle Application

The Rule: If the dog is in pain and you need to manipulate the injury, muzzle. No exceptions for "nice dogs."

Proper Fitting: Snug enough that the jaw cannot open, loose enough for panting.

Brachycephalic breeds (Pugs, Bulldogs): Use basket muzzles only; cloth can obstruct breathing.

Proper muzzle fitting ensures safety while allowing the dog to breathe comfortably

ü™¢ Makeshift Muzzle Construction

  1. Create a loop with gauze, a leash, or a strip of cloth
  2. Loop over muzzle, tighten behind the canine teeth
  3. Cross ends under the jaw
  4. Tie behind the ears (not the throat)
CRITICAL: Never muzzle a vomiting dog or one with respiratory distress. If the dog starts vomiting, cut the muzzle off immediately with safety scissors.

üõ°Ô∏è Restraint for Treatment

Lateral Recumbency

  • Place the dog on its side on a flat, stable surface
  • Stand with your body alongside the dog's spine
  • One arm under the neck (controlling head), one arm over the body, securing the legs
  • Weight is distributed to prevent rolling but not compressing the chest

The Standing Hold

  • Stand beside the dog, facing the same direction
  • One arm around the chest (under front legs), one arm around the abdomen (in front of hind legs)
  • Pull dog closely against your body ‚Äî this mimics the "pressure hug" and has a calming effect via the parasympathetic nervous system

ü©∫ Assessment Techniques

Heart Rate & Pulse

Femoral pulse: Groin area where hind leg meets body; use two fingers, not thumb.

Apical pulse: Place stethoscope just behind left elbow at 4th-5th intercostal space.

Pulse quality: Should be strong, regular, and synchronous with heartbeat.

Body Temperature Technique

  1. Lubricate digital thermometer with petroleum jelly
  2. Insert gently into rectum 1 inch (small dogs) to 2 inches (large dogs)
  3. Wait for beep; do not force if resistance occurs

Examining gum color and mucous membranes is a critical assessment skill

üëÑ Normal Gum Colors by Breed Type

Breed Category Examples Normal Gum Color
Highly Pigmented Chow Chow, Shar-Pei, Thai Ridgeback Blue-black, purple-black, lavender-gray
Spotted/Patchy Labrador, Rottweiler, Mastiff, Dalmatian, GSD Mixed pink and black patches
Light Pink Golden Retriever, Beagle, Poodle, Greyhound Salmon-pink
How to Assess Dark-Pigmented Breeds: If your dog has naturally black/blue gums (Chow Chow, Shar-Pei), check these alternative mucous membrane sites:
  1. Inner Lip (Buccal Mucosa): Lift the lip and look at the inner surface opposite the gums
  2. Conjunctiva: Gently pull down the lower eyelid ‚Äî should be pink
  3. Tongue: Often less pigmented than gums in spotted breeds
  4. Vulva/Prepuce: Mucous membranes of the genitalia (if safe to check)

üö® Pathological Gum Colors (All Breeds)

These colors indicate medical emergencies regardless of breed. Check alternative sites if your dog has naturally dark gums.

Color Medical Term Indicates Level
Pale Pink/White Pallor Anemia, blood loss, shock Emergency
Blue/Purple Cyanosis Oxygen deprivation Immediate
Yellow Icterus Liver failure, hemolysis Urgent (24h)
Brick Red Hyperemia Heatstroke, sepsis, shock Emergency
Brown/Gray Necrosis Tissue death, severe toxicity Critical
Take a photo of your dog's normal gum color when healthy. In an emergency, compare to this baseline rather than trying to remember "how dark is too dark."

üíß Dehydration Assessment

The Skin Tent Test

  1. Lift skin over shoulders/scapula into a "tent"
  2. Normal: Snaps back immediately
  3. 5-7% dehydrated: Returns slowly (2-3 seconds)
  4. >10% dehydrated: Remains tented (CRITICAL)

Other Indicators

  • Dry/sticky gums
  • Sunken eyes
  • Prolonged CRT

First Aid for Life-Threatening Emergencies

Critical Interventions That Save Lives

CPR training showing proper hand placement and compression technique

ü´Å CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation)

Based on 2024 RECOVER Initiatives Guidelines
When to Perform

Dog is unresponsive, apneic (not breathing), AND has no pulse. Do NOT perform on a dog that has a pulse or is breathing.

Compression Technique (2024 Standards)

  • Rate: 100-120 compressions per minute (steady rhythm ‚Äî think "Stayin' Alive")
  • Depth: 1/3 to 1/2 of chest width
  • Recoil: Allow full chest recoil between compressions (50:50 ratio)
  • Ratio: 30 compressions, then 2 breaths
Dog Type Position Technique
Most dogs Left lateral recumbency Compress over heart (behind left elbow)
Barrel-chested (Bulldogs, Labs) Dorsal recumbency (on back) Compress over sternum
Deep-chested (Greyhounds) Left lateral Compress highest point of ribcage
Small dogs/cats (<6.8 kgs) Left lateral Circumferential 2-thumb technique
Rotation: Switch compressors every 2 minutes to prevent fatigue and maintain quality.

‚ö° Shock

Circulatory collapse prevents oxygen delivery to tissues.

Causes

  • Blood loss (hemorrhagic)
  • Allergic reaction (anaphylactic)
  • Infection (septic)
  • Trauma, GDV, heatstroke

Signs

  • Pale gums
  • Rapid/weak pulse
  • Cold extremities
  • Lethargy, collapse

First Aid Protocol

1

Position

Left lateral recumbency, head slightly lower than body (if no head trauma)

2

Warmth

Cover with blankets (prevent hypothermia but don't overheat)

3

Oxygen

If available, flow-by oxygen near the nose (not forced)

4

Transport

Minimal handling, gentle movement, immediate veterinary transfer

NO FLUIDS BY MOUTH ‚Äî aspiration risk if vomiting; will need IV fluids at the hospital.

üòÆ Choking

Recognizing choking signs: pawing at mouth, distress, inability to breathe

Recognition: Pawing at mouth, cyanotic (blue) tongue, high-pitched squeaking, inability to bark/cough effectively.
1

Open Mouth

Use one hand on upper jaw, thumb of other hand on lower jaw behind canines ‚Äî forces mouth open without being bitten.

2

Finger Sweep

Look with flashlight, remove object with hooked finger (sweep only if you see it ‚Äî blind sweeps push objects deeper).

3

If Still Obstructed

Small dogs: Hold by hips, head down, shake gently with centrifugal force.
Large dogs: Lift hind legs like a wheelbarrow; gravity may dislodge object.

4

Heimlich (Last Resort)

Place fist just behind ribcage, thrust sharply up toward head (only if breathing has ceased).

Post-obstruction: Even if successful, see a vet ‚Äî objects can cause esophageal tears or residual aspiration pneumonia.

ü©∏ Bleeding

Proper bandaging technique for wound management

Type Appearance Response
Capillary (oozing) Slow, oozing Clean, antibiotic ointment, bandage
Venous (steady flow) Dark red, steady Direct pressure 3-5 min; pressure bandage
Arterial (spurting) Bright red, rhythmic LIFE-THREATENING ‚Äî direct pressure, tourniquet, emergency transport

Bandaging Technique

  1. Non-stick gauze directly on wound
  2. Rolled gauze wrap (snug, not tight ‚Äî two fingers should fit under)
  3. Self-adhering outer layer (Vetrap)
  4. Check toes every 15 min ‚Äî if cold/swollen, bandage too tight
Tourniquet Protocol (Limb Arterial Bleeding Only)
  • Place 2-3 inches above wound (never over joint)
  • Use belt, leash, or cloth with stick/pencil for windlass
  • Tighten until bleeding stops
  • CRITICAL: Loosen for 30 seconds every 5 minutes to prevent tissue necrosis
  • Mark time of application on dog with Sharpie

üÜò Emergency Protocol

1

Identify

Find packaging/remaining substance; note time and approximate amount

2

Call

Poison Control or Pet Poison Helpline AND your vet

3

Do NOT Induce Vomiting If:

  • Caustic substances (acids/alkalis)
  • Hydrocarbons (petroleum products)
  • Unconscious/altered mental status
  • Sharp objects (bone, glass)
  • History of GDV/bloat-prone breeds
4

Induce Vomiting (Only if Advised)

3% hydrogen peroxide: 1 teaspoon per 10 lbs, max 3 tablespoons; works within 15 minutes

Great Danes and other deep-chested breeds are at highest risk for gastric dilatation-volvulus (bloat)

üêï Canine Bloat (GDV) ‚Äî The Mother of All Emergencies

100% Fatal Without Treatment

10-45% mortality even with immediate surgery. Time is tissue ‚Äî stomach walls necrose within hours.

Classic Presentation

  • Unproductive retching (nothing comes up)
  • Distended abdomen (tight as a drum)
  • Restlessness, pacing
  • Prayer position (hind up, chest down)
  • Pale gums, rapid heart rate, collapse

High-Risk Breeds

  • Great Danes (highest risk)
  • Weimaraners, Standard Poodles
  • Irish Setters, German Shepherds
  • Dobermans, Basset Hounds, Boxers
DO NOT: Attempt to relieve gas with needles at home (risk of perforation), give simethicone (too slow), or wait to see if it resolves.
Immediate Action: Transport immediately (call ahead; they need to prep surgery suite). Minimize movement. Position sternum down in vehicle.

Environmental Emergencies

Heatstroke, Hypothermia, Burns & Water Safety

Excessive panting and drooling are early signs of heatstroke - a true emergency

üå°Ô∏è Heatstroke

Lethal Zone: >42.8¬∞C (109¬∞F) ‚Äî mortality approaches 100%

Risk Factors

  • Brachycephalic breeds (Pugs, Bulldogs)
  • Obesity, dark/thick coats
  • Exertion in heat
  • Enclosed cars (60¬∞C/140¬∞F in 40 min at 27¬∞C/80¬∞F)

Progressive Signs

  • Early: Excessive panting, bright red gums
  • Progressive: Vomiting/diarrhea (bloody), ataxia
  • Shock stage: Pale gums, bradycardia, coma

First Aid

1
Remove from heat: Shade/air conditioning immediately
2
Wet down: Lukewarm (not cold) water on belly, groin, armpits, paw pads
3
Airflow: Fan aggressively
4
Water: Small amounts if conscious (don't force)
5
STOP cooling at 39.4¬∞C (103¬∞F) to prevent hypothermia rebound
CRITICAL: Do NOT use ice water or ice baths ‚Äî peripheral vasoconstriction traps heat in core organs.

Cold weather emergencies: monitor for shivering, lethargy, and frostbite on extremities

‚ùÑÔ∏è Hypothermia & Frostbite

Stage Temperature Signs
Mild 32.2-37.2¬∞C (90-99¬∞F) Shivering, lethargy, weakness
Moderate 27.78-32.22¬∞C (82-90¬∞F) Muscle rigidity, decreased heart rate
Severe <27.78¬∞C (<82¬∞F) Fixed dilated pupils, coma, ventricular fibrillation
Frostbite Sites: Ear tips, tail, paws, scrotum. DO NOT: Rub tissue (ice crystals damage cells further); do not apply direct heat (heating pads cause thermal burns to numb tissue).

üî• Burns

Classification

First degree: Red, painful, no blistering
Second degree: Blisters, hair loss
Third degree: Charring, white/gray tissue, painless

First Aid

  1. Stop the burning: Remove from source
  2. Cool: Room temperature water flush 10-20 min
  3. Cover: Sterile, non-stick dressing
Electrical Burns: Mouth lesions may look minor, but pulmonary edema develops 24-48 hours later. Immediate vet check required.

Water safety: Monitor for both drowning and water intoxication (hyponatremia)

üíß Drowning & Water Intoxication

Water Intoxication (Hyponatremia): Ingesting large volumes of fresh water leads to dilution of blood sodium and cerebral edema. Differentiation from heatstroke: Usually occurs in cool/cold water; no hyperthermia.

Action: Emergency vet for IV hypertonic saline ‚Äî death can occur within hours; do NOT restrict water (causes rapid sodium shifts).

Wound Care

Abrasions, Bite Wounds, and Puncture Wounds

Proper wound cleaning and bandaging prevents infection and promotes healing

ü©π Abrasions (Scrapes)

  • Clean with sterile saline or dilute betadine
  • Apply thin layer of antibiotic ointment
  • Cover with non-stick pad; change daily
  • Do not scrub vigorously (damages granulation tissue)

ü¶∑ Puncture Wounds (Nails, Sticks, Bites)

Worse than they look: Seal quickly, trap infection, anaerobic bacteria thrive.
  • Do not flush deeply at home (can force bacteria deeper)
  • Clip hair, clean surface, leave open to drain
  • Bite wounds: 100% infection rate within 12 hours; antibiotics mandatory
  • Pocket abscess: Hot packing (warm compress 10 min, 4x daily) to bring to head

‚úã Degloving Injuries

Wrap in saline-soaked sterile gauze, keep moist ‚Äî dry tissue dies. Emergency surgical intervention required.

Preventing Emergencies

A Holistic Approach to Accident Prevention

Prevention through training, environmental management, and regular veterinary care

üè† Dog-Proofing & Environmental Management

The "Crawl Walk": Get on hands and knees to dog eye-level; remove electrical cords, small objects, secure trash with carabiners.

Kitchen Protocol

  • Xylitol scan: Check peanut butter labels
  • Grapes/raisins: Secure in closed cabinets
  • Compost: Secure lids (mycotoxins)
  • Bread dough: Rising dough produces ethanol

Garage/Hazmat

  • Ethylene glycol (antifreeze): Use propylene glycol alternatives
  • Pest control: Bait stations, not loose pellets

üöó Car Safety

The Physics: At 45 km/hr crash, 28 kg dog becomes ~1225 kg projectile.
  • Airbags kill dogs in front seats (even crated)
  • Truck beds: Eye injuries, ejection, heatstroke
  • Use crash-tested crates (Gunner Kennels, Variocage) or harnesses (Sleepypod Clickit)

üéí The 72-Hour Disaster Kit

  • 3 days of food/water (rotate every 3 months)
  • Medications (30-day supply if possible)
  • Vaccination records (waterproof bag)
  • Recent photo (proof of ownership)
  • Sturdy leashes/harnesses and collapsible crates
  • First aid kit
  • Sanitation supplies
